Pairs Trading with Asset Ratios¶

I. Motivation¶

(a). Copper / Gold v.s. 10 Year Treasury¶

Copper gold ratio is often regarded as a very relavant indicator for understanding the 10 year treasury yield moves (there is indeed some correlation and I'll list below). The intutition is clear: copper is a very useful kind of industrial material. It is essential to the economic engine and thus the demand / price of which can be used to gauge the health of the ecnonomy. If things are booming, people buy a lot of copper and its price picks up. If there's a recession, the contrary is true. While for gold the opposite usually holds as it is one of the popular safe heaven assets. So the ratio of copper, gold prices is procyclical. Now we also know something about 10 year treasury. It is also considered as a kind of safe asset so the yield of which is therefore procyclical as well. Intuitively speaking, these two indicators should have some relavance with each other:

(b). Copper / Gold v.s. 30 Year Treasury / 10 Year Treasury¶

There are other things we can say about the copper gold ratio too. We may consider Copper as a kind of expected future cash flows as people buy it because they think they can use it to produce something that can bring in money in the future. For gold, as mentioned in the assginment document, it can be considered as a zero coupon bond -- or a kind of risk-free discounting factor. So taking the ratio of copper to gold is trying to get something like "discounted future economic expectation".

Now if we replace copper with 30 year treausry and gold with 10 year treasury, we may reach to a similar kind of conclusion.

And the two ratios also have a high correlation like above and they are of similar scale:

So this introduces the idea of monitoring the relative positions of the spread time series and betting on its mean-reverting behavior.

II. Some Statistics¶

In this section I'm going to do some more rigorous tests on the two ratio time series. But first, I have to introduce a bit of theories on cointegration.

Please see page 153 ~ 212 in the attached slides on the introduction of unit-root nonstationarity and cointegration. It also talks about how to test for cointegration relationship and if there exists one, how to use Error Correction Model to extract the linear stationary components (cointegrating vectors). These are the theoretical basis for the analysis or test that follows.

If you are familiar with the concepts, you can just skip them and jump to the tests part.

(a). Cointegration¶

As sketched out in the slides, the first step checking the cointegration relation between two time series is to do some unit-root tests on each individual time seires. The point being, if they are statinary already, one can just study these series directly. The usual situation would be both time series are non-stationary, something like I(1) processes (or, random walks) and taking the first difference would make them stationary. If this is the case, one can then proceed to check if there's a linear combination of these two that is also stationary, i.e.: if they are cointegrated. The test I will be using here is the augmented Engle-Granger two-step cointegration test. It's a standard procedure and has been implemented in Python statsmodels.

(a). ECM¶

Now that they are cointegrated, what's left to do is to extract the cointegrating vector and double check it is indeed stationary. Here I use the Error Correction Model for the combined 2 dimensional process $R_{t}$:

$$ \Delta R_{t} = \mu_t + \Pi R_{t-1} + \Phi_1^* \Delta R_{t-1} + ... + \Phi_p^* \Delta R_{t - p + 1} + \epsilon_{t} \tag{1} $$

where $\Pi$ admits a decomposition $\Pi = \alpha \beta^{'}$.

So we want to find $\beta$, or more precisely, find $w_t = \beta^{'} R_{t}$:

III. Strategy¶

So the idea is very straightforward. We model the spread of ratios as a mean-reverting time series and continuously monitor its values. If someday it breaks the upper non-trivial bound, we short the spread, and when it comes back to the trivial bound in the future, we close the position. And vice versa. By trivial or non-trivial bounds, I mean:

$$ mean:\ \mu,\ standard\ deviation:\ std \\ trivial\ bounds:\ [\mu - 0.5 * std,\ \mu + 0.5 * std] \\ one\ sigma\ bounds:\ [\mu - std,\ \mu + std] \\ two\ sigma\ bounds:\ [\mu - 2 * std,\ \mu + 2 * std] \\ three\ sigma\ bounds:\ [\mu - 3 * std,\ \mu + 3 * std] \\ $$

When the spread lives in the trivial bounds, we do nothing, when it for instance breaks the upper trivial bound and continues moving upward and breaks the one sigma upper bound, we open 1 sythetic short position, if two sigma bound then 2 sythetic positions and etc. I have no time to plot out the charts here but I hope this has lived in your imagination.

There's one difficulty that exists here. That is about the spread. Its's a difference of ratios, it's something non-tradable. I figured out a way to get around this by using first order approximation to produce a some kind of synthetic trade that replicates 1 unit of spread:

Let $f(a, b, c, d) = \frac{a}{b} - \frac{c}{d}$, take the first order approximation to be:

$$ df(a, b, c, d) = f_{a} da + f_{b} db + f_{c} dc + f_{d} dd $$

To produce 1 unit of $f$, we can take $\frac{1}{4f_{a}}$ a, $\frac{1}{4f_{b}}$ b, $\frac{1}{4f_{c}}$ c, $\frac{1}{4f_{d}}$ d. All derivatives can be calculated correspondingly. For example, let 10 year yield be $y$, 30 year be $z$ and Modefied Duration be ModdD, we have:

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial P} = \frac{\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}}{\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}} = - \frac{1}{P*ModD*z_{t}} $$

And I took $\frac{1}{4}$ purey for the simplicity. One can of course say replace this number with some kind of function of the rolling correlations between $f$ and the individual contributor. But that is too complex and not necessary here since the synthesis itself is largely an approximation because I have ommitted all those higher order derivatives and one can hardly argue that these four assets live in four independent spaces.
